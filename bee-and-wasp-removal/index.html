Signs You Might Need Professional Bee and Wasp Removal

It's not always a giant, obvious nest that indicates a problem. Sometimes, the signs are more subtle. Being able to recognize these indicators can help you address an infestation before it becomes a major issue. Ignoring early signs can lead to larger colonies, increased risk of stings, and potential damage to your property. If you notice any of the following, it's wise to consider contacting a professional for a proper assessment.

Frequent Sightings of Bees or Wasps

Seeing a lone bee or wasp occasionally is normal, especially during warmer months. However, if you begin to regularly see a significant number of these insects around a specific area of your property, it could indicate the presence of a nearby nest. Pay attention to flight patterns. Are they consistently entering or leaving a particular hole, crack, or cavity? This consistent activity is a strong indicator of a nesting site. Different species have different flight characteristics and preferred nesting locations. Observing their behavior can provide valuable clues. For instance, yellowjackets are often seen scavenging around garbage cans or picnic areas, while paper wasps are more likely to build umbrella-shaped nests under eaves.

Visible Nests or Hives on Your Property

This is perhaps the most obvious sign of an infestation. Nests and hives can vary greatly in appearance and location depending on the species. Hornet nests are often large, football-shaped structures made of a papery material, typically found hanging from trees or structures. Paper wasp nests are open, umbrella-shaped combs, often found under eaves, porch ceilings, or in sheds. Yellowjackets often build nests in the ground, in wall voids, or in other enclosed spaces. Don't attempt to remove a visible nest yourself, as this can be extremely dangerous and provoke the colony into defense mode. A professional has the knowledge and equipment to safely handle such situations.

Buzzing Sounds Within Walls or Structures

Sometimes, bees or wasps will build nests inside wall voids, attics, or other hidden areas. In these cases, you might not see a visible nest but may hear persistent buzzing sounds coming from within the structure. These sounds can indicate a growing colony within your home or business. Ignoring these sounds can lead to the colony expanding and potentially causing structural damage or attracting other pests. Identifying the exact location of the buzzing can be tricky, but our experienced technicians have methods for pinpointing the source of the activity. Ant control or spider removal might deal with visible pests, but buzzing behind walls requires a different investigative approach.

Increased Aggression or Defensive Behavior

Bees and wasps are generally more defensive when their nest is established and growing, or when they feel threatened. If you notice that these insects are becoming more aggressive or readily defending a specific area, it's a strong indication that you are near a nest. This is a critical time to avoid the area and contact professionals. Trying to swat at them or disturb the nest can provoke a mass attack, which can be very dangerous, especially for individuals with allergies. Their defensive perimeter expands as the colony grows, making areas that were once safe now potentially hazardous.

Seeing Mud Tunnels or Other Structures

While not strictly bees or wasps, it’s worth noting the presence of related insects like mud daubers, which build mud tubes or nests, typically on sheltered surfaces. While generally less aggressive than social wasps or hornets, their nests can still be unsightly and indicate insect activity that might warrant inspection. Knowing the different types of stinging insects and their nesting behaviors is part of a comprehensive pest management strategy. Understanding Different Types of Stinging Insects in Commerce City, CO

Commerce City, like many areas in Colorado, is home to a variety of stinging insects. Knowing the difference between them is important, as it affects the best approach for removal and understanding their behavior and potential risks. While many people lump all stinging insects together, recognizing the specific type can influence the urgency and method of intervention. Some are more aggressive than others, and some, like certain bees, are vital pollinators and should be protected if possible.

Yellowjackets

Yellowjackets are a type of social wasp and are known for being particularly aggressive, especially in late summer and fall when their colonies are at their peak. They are often mistaken for bees due to their yellow and black coloration, but they are more slender and have a distinct waist. Yellowjackets frequently build nests in the ground, in wall voids, under eaves, or in other protected cavities. They are attracted to sweets and proteins and are often seen scavenging around garbage cans, picnics, and outdoor dining areas. Their stings can be painful and, like other stinging insects, can be dangerous to individuals with allergies. Dealing with yellowjackets almost always requires professional intervention due to their aggressive nature and the hidden locations of their nests.

Hornets

Hornets, such as the Bald-faced Hornet (which is actually a type of yellowjacket), are large, social wasps known for building large, paper-like nests often suspended from trees or structures. They are also highly defensive of their nests and will aggressively sting if they feel threatened. Hornet stings are often more painful than those of other wasps. Their nests can grow quite large over the summer months, housing thousands of individuals. Due to the size and location of hornet nests, and the aggressive nature of these insects, professional removal is essential. Attempting to remove a hornet nest without proper equipment and knowledge is extremely risky.

Paper Wasps

Paper wasps are another common type of social wasp. They build umbrella-shaped nests made of a papery material, typically in sheltered locations like under eaves, porch ceilings, sheds, or attics. While generally less aggressive than yellowjackets or hornets, paper wasps will sting if their nest is disturbed. They are often seen around gardens, feeding on nectar and insects. Their nests are usually smaller than hornet nests, but a large colony can still pose a significant threat, particularly near high-traffic areas. Early detection and removal of paper wasp nests are advisable to prevent the colony from growing larger and more defensive. Understanding their nesting habits helps in both removal and preventative measures.

Bees (Honeybees, Bumblebees, etc.)

While our service includes bee removal, we differentiate between aggressive wasp species and more docile, often beneficial, bee species. Honeybees, for instance, are vital pollinators and are generally not aggressive unless their hive is directly threatened. We prioritize safe removal and relocation of honeybee hives whenever possible, working with local beekeepers to preserve these important insects. However, swarming bees or established hives in problematic locations still require professional handling. Bumblebees are also important pollinators and are generally gentle, though they can sting if provoked. Their nests are often found in the ground or in thick vegetation. Knowing the difference between a honeybee hive and a yellowjacket nest is crucial for selecting the appropriate removal strategy.

The Importance of Identification

Correctly identifying the type of stinging insect is the first and most critical step in addressing an infestation. Different species have different behaviors, nesting habits, and levels of aggression, all of which influence the safest and most effective removal strategy. Mistaken identity can lead to using incorrect methods, potentially agitating the colony and increasing the risk of stings, or harming beneficial insects. High Swarm Activity

Seeing a large cluster of bees or wasps flying together, often in a swirling mass, is known as swarming. Swarming is a natural part of the reproductive cycle for some social insects, particularly honeybees, where a portion of the colony leaves the original nest to establish a new one. While swarms are often docile as they search for a new home, a swarm settling on your property can be concerning and indicate a new hive is attempting to form. Professional help is needed to safely remove and, if possible, relocate the swarm. Swarming typically occurs in the spring or early summer.

Evidence of Chewing or Wood Dust

For carpenter bees, one of the key signs is the presence of half-inch diameter holes in wooden surfaces, often with piles of sawdust or frass below the entrance. These are the entry points to their tunnels and nesting galleries. Over time, extensive tunneling can weaken the structural integrity of the wood. If you notice these holes or sawdust, it's a sign of carpenter bee activity that needs to be addressed to prevent further damage. Unexplained Stings

If you or members of your household or staff are experiencing unexplained stings, it's a strong indicator that there is an active nest or increased stinging insect presence on your property that you may not have located yet. Pay attention to where and when the stings are occurring to help narrow down the possible location of the nest.

Prevention Strategies for Bees and Wasps

Once the current infestation is handled, the next step is to make your property less appealing to future bee and wasp activity. Implementing preventative measures can significantly reduce the likelihood of another infestation. While it's impossible to completely eliminate the presence of bees and wasps from your environment (nor would we want to, given their ecological importance), you can make your property a less attractive nesting site. This involves a combination of simple maintenance tasks and being mindful of potential attractants. A little effort in prevention goes a long way in avoiding the stress and expense of future removals. Seal Cracks and Crevices

Bees and wasps often build nests in protected cavities, including cracks and crevices in the exterior of your home or business. Regularly inspect your property for any gaps in siding, around windows and doors, in the foundation, or around utility penetrations. Seal these openings with caulk or other appropriate sealing materials. This eliminates potential entry points and nesting sites. Pay close attention to eaves, soffits, and areas where different building materials meet. Even small gaps can provide access for stinging insects looking for shelter.

Repair Damaged Wood

As mentioned earlier, carpenter bees are attracted to soft or rotting wood. Inspect your decks, fences, eaves, and other wooden structures for any signs of damage or deterioration. Repair or replace damaged wood to make it less appealing to these wood-boring insects. Applying a fresh coat of paint or stain can also help protect the wood surface and make it less attractive as a nesting site. Addressing any signs of squirrel removal that might have caused initial damage to wood is also a good preventative step.

Trim Bushes and Trees

Overgrown vegetation can provide sheltered locations for wasps to build nests. Keep bushes, trees, and shrubs trimmed and away from the exterior of your home or business. This reduces potential nesting sites and makes it easier to spot any potential activity. Regular landscaping maintenance is not only aesthetically pleasing but also a practical step in pest prevention. This also helps with mosquito control by reducing harborage areas.

Secure Garbage Cans

Yellowjackets and other wasps are scavengers and are highly attracted to food waste. Ensure that your garbage cans have tight-fitting lids and are kept clean. Rinse out food containers before discarding them. Keep garbage cans a reasonable distance from entryways and outdoor living areas. Regularly emptying and cleaning your garbage cans will make them less appealing to foraging wasps. This is a simple but effective preventative measure.

Clean Up Fallen Fruit

If you have fruit trees on your property, make sure to regularly clean up any fallen fruit. Decomposing fruit is a significant attractant for wasps. Promptly removing this food source can help deter stinging insects from lingering around your yard. This is a seasonal task but an important one during fruit bearing times.

Properly Store Pet Food

Open bags or containers of pet food can also attract wasps and other pests. Store pet food in airtight containers, preferably indoors. Clean up any spills promptly. This denies foraging insects an easy food source. This applies to both residential and commercial properties, especially those dealing with food service.
